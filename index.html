Overview

This site presents a digitized version of the Universal Language of John Wilkins, published in An Essay Towards a Real Character, and a Philosophical Language in 1668. Fired with the spirit of the Early Modern period, John Wilkins attempted to document the full range of things in the world (from beetles to metaphysical categories) by means of a new language, where the words themselves would reflect the things they represented.

To use an example cited by Jorge Luis Borges, de means element, deb means the first of the elements (fire), and deba means the first type of fire (flame). Wilkins also developed characters to represent each word, which were constructed in a similar way.

For more information on the categorization scheme, please see these slides (PDF or PowerPoint); this is a shortened version of a conference presentation that I gave at "On Digital Pasts and Digital Futures," California State University Long Beach, in April 2023. Links to several excellent academic essays on the language are below.

Finally, please note that page presents every noun note in the text, from which adjectives and adverbs can be derived. This page does not include pronouns, prepositions, or the construction of sentences, which Wikins describes starting on page 417.

How to Use This Site

On the Dictionary page, you may type in any of the 17,000+ words listed in Wilkins' text, to see the translation into the two forms of Wilkins' language (in Latin letters and his own characters.) You may also click a link to hear how the word should be pronounced. The Dictionary page also summarizes where the word appears in Wilkins' classification scheme and provides links to the word in the original text, as well as dictionary definitions. Note that the list only includes words that appear in Wilkins' text you won t find keyboard or bullet train.

The Additional Forms page presents the thirty-six variations that Wilkins describes for every word (see the middle of page 443 in the original text): concrete/abstract, substantive/adjective/adverb, neutral/active/passive, and singular/plural.

The Character Map page presents every character, also shown on page 443.

The Categorization Scheme page shows the upper categories of Wilkins' classification scheme: eleven chapters, forty genera, and two hundred and fifty differences. The next level is Species (for which Wilkins does not provide names), and then the individual words. For more information on the categorization scheme, please see the resources below.

The Published Table reproduces the foldout table that appears on pages 442-443 in the text. However, there are two issues: first, Wilkins' published table does not include all of his words (likely for reasons of length). Second, the published table differs from the text in several places, which are indicated on this site.

The Full Digital Table is table that arranges every main entry in in the text, showing how an ideal form of the published table might look.

Resources

The Original Text

There are at least three facsimiles of the published text on Google Books:

  • National Library of the Czech Republic
  • Lyon Public Library
  • Bavarian State Library

Of these, that of the National Library of the Czech Republic is generally the easiest to read. Note that the published table is either incomplete or missing from these versions; a scan of these pages is available on this site.

This website was adapted from the digitized text published by the University of Michigan Library. This project would not have been possible without this enormously useful resource.
